Choices

Who Made These Choices and Why It Matters

The risks that we have outlined are not simply features of the technology — they are the result of choices. Understanding who makes those choices, what alternatives exist, and what frameworks might guide better decisions is the core of any serious engagement with the ethics of LEO satellite broadband.

SpaceX chose to launch Starlink at scale rapidly, before comprehensive international frameworks for managing orbital congestion were in place. This was a commercially rational decision — moving quickly secured spectrum rights, established market presence, and created network effects that are difficult for later entrants to overcome. But it was a choice, not an inevitability, and it created facts on the ground — or rather, in orbit — that now constrain what other stakeholders can do. Governments and international bodies largely allowed this to happen, either because regulatory frameworks didn't anticipate the scale of commercial LEO deployment or because the economic and strategic interests involved made aggressive regulation unattractive.

For engineers and technical professionals working on these systems, professional codes of ethics are directly relevant. The ACM Code of Ethics calls on computing professionals to consider the public good, to be honest and trustworthy, and to avoid harm — including hard-to-reverse changes to shared environments. These aren't abstract principles: they create real obligations to raise concerns about long-term orbital sustainability, to push back when short-term commercial pressure overrides longer-term thinking, and to be honest with policymakers and the public about what the technology can and cannot deliver. The choice to remain silent when aware of significant systemic risks is itself a choice.

What Better Choices Look Like: Regulation, Policy and Community

At the regulatory level, there are meaningful decisions still to be made. International coordination on debris mitigation standards — including requirements for satellite lifespan, deorbit timelines, and collision avoidance capabilities — is technically achievable but requires political will to implement. The ITU (International Telecommunication Union) governs spectrum allocation and has some role in orbital slot coordination, but its frameworks were not designed for constellations of thousands of satellites and are under pressure to adapt. The choice of whether to strengthen these frameworks, and how quickly, will shape the sustainability of the orbital environment.

At the policy level, the choice of how to deploy LEO broadband for public benefit rather than purely private profit involves subsidy design, procurement policy, and community engagement. Countries that have simply left deployment to the market have seen uneven outcomes — the economics of satellite broadband still tend to favour users who can pay, and communities most in need of access are not always the most commercially attractive to serve. Policy choices around who gets subsidies, how community access hubs are funded, and what data sovereignty requirements apply to satellite services used by public institutions all shape whether the technology's equity potential is realised.

For end users and communities, the relevant choices include whether to advocate for better regulation, how to engage with service providers about pricing and terms, and how to frame the technology in local infrastructure planning. Framing LEO broadband as a complement to rather than a substitute for fibre investment is a choice with significant long-term implications — communities that accept satellite service as sufficient may find their case for fibre infrastructure weakened, even though the long-term trajectory of fibre performance and cost is more favourable. The tension at the heart of this technology — between genuine social benefit and real systemic risks — does not resolve itself. It requires ongoing choices by engineers, policymakers, companies, and communities about what kind of future they are building toward.
