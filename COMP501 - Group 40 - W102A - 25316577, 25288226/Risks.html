Risks

Performance and Accessibility: The Immediate Practical Risks

Any honest account of LEO satellite broadband has to engage with genuine risks some specific to current systems, others raising longer-term concerns about sustainability, fairness, and unintended consequences.

**Variable and unreliable performance is the most immediate practical issue. Unlike fibre, Starlink performance fluctuates significantly depending on conditions. Weather degrades signal strength; heavy rain or dense cloud cover can cause outages. Terrain matters the dish requires a clear sky view, and locations with nearby trees, buildings, or steep hillsides experience regular interruptions as satellites pass behind obstructions. Time of day and local user density affect available capacity. Measurement studies consistently find a spread of performance outcomes rather than a single reliable figure. While median performance has improved as the constellation has grown, this variability is a real limitation for applications requiring consistent, predictable connectivity and there is a meaningful risk of overselling the technology to underserved communities and then failing to deliver on those promises.

Orbital debris and long-term space sustainability are arguably the most serious risks. Each Starlink satellite has an operational lifespan of roughly four to six years, after which it deorbits. With over 7,000 satellites currently operational and plans for tens of thousands more from Starlink and competing constellations, low Earth orbit is becoming significantly more congested. The danger is not merely the presence of functioning satellites, but the potential for collisions. A collision between two objects in LEO generates a cloud of debris fragments travelling at orbital velocity, each capable of damaging or destroying other satellites. In the scenario described by researcher Donald Kessler known as Kessler Syndrome a cascading chain of collisions could render certain orbital altitudes unusable for generations. While this remains a tail risk rather than an imminent outcome, the decisions being made now about constellation scale, satellite design, and end-of-life disposal will shape the orbital environment for decades. This is a case where the costs of getting it wrong are large, hard to reverse, and borne by everyone including future users who had no say in the decisions.

Orbital Debris and Astronomy: The Environmental Risks

Impacts on astronomy are a more immediate and less speculative harm. The bright streaks left by Starlink satellites across long-exposure astronomical images have been well documented and widely criticised by the scientific community. Ground-based telescopes — including those used to detect near-Earth asteroids and conduct wide-field sky surveys — have had observations degraded or contaminated by satellite trails. SpaceX has made efforts to reduce satellite brightness through design changes, with partial success, but the fundamental issue of thousands of fast-moving objects crossing the sky persists. This is a case where the commercial interests of one organisation create a negative externality for a separate community scientific researchers who had no meaningful voice in the decision to launch at scale.

Cost and accessibility barriers** undercut the equity case. Hardware costs for a Starlink terminal and ongoing monthly subscription fees remain significant in precisely the communities where the access gap is most acute. A service that is technically available from orbit but practically unaffordable for the intended beneficiaries doesn't fully deliver on its promise. Without deliberate policy intervention subsidy programmes, community access models, digital literacy support the gap between theoretical and actual benefit can be substantial.

Concentration of Control: The Systemic Risk

Concentration of control is a less-discussed but important systemic risk. A small number of private companies — primarily SpaceX — currently dominate LEO satellite broadband. The infrastructure these companies are building is becoming critical for communities with no alternative. Questions of pricing, service terms, data sovereignty, and continuity of service are therefore significant, and the leverage available to affected communities or governments is limited when alternatives are absent.
